Chapter One

Introduction to Learning and Assessment

I think animal testing is a terrible idea; they get all nervouse and give the wrong answers. —A Bit of Fry & Laurie

People, too, sometimes give the wrong answers on tests because they get nervous and scared. That's what often happened to Jacob. Tests scared the pants off him. When he sat down to take one, he would quickly forget every sensible thought and well-memorized fact he had stuffed into his brain. His test results were a disaster. They greatly chagrined his mother—especially when, following a series of countywide achievement tests, the principal called her in.

"Jacob should probably stop dreaming about going to university and becoming a professor," said the principal. "There's other stuff he can do."

But Jacob didn't stop dreaming. He just kept trying to educate himself. "They can't teach me more stuff just by giving me more tests," he declared. "That's about as sensible as thinking you can make yourself thinner just by weighing yourself a lot."

1.1 Introduction

Focus Question

What is this Chapter about?

Determining the best assessment procedures and instruments is no simple matter and is thus not without controversy. Although educators and parents do not always agree on these matters, there is general consensus about the characteristics of good measuring instruments.

Most important is that evaluative instruments be fair and that students see them as being fair.

The most common student complaint about tests and testing practices has to do with their imagined or real lack of fairness (Bouville, 2008; Takayama, 2018). The importance of test fairness was highlighted during the Vietnam War in the 1960s. President John F. Kennedy's decision to send troops to Vietnam led large numbers of age-eligible men to be drafted as soldiers, some of whom died or were seriously injured in the war. Men who went to college were usually exempt from the draft—or their required military service was at least deferred. So, to avoid being drafted, it became crucial for many young men to be admitted to undergraduate or postgraduate studies. For some, passing college or graduate entrance exams was literally a matter of life and death. Therefore, it was vital that the admission exams be as fair as possible.

Just how fair are our educational assessments? We don't always know. Yet science provides ways to define and sometimes actually measure the characteristics of tests.

For example, the best assessment instruments have three important qualities:

  1. fairness
  2. validity
  3. reliability

Summative Assessment

Assessment that is mainly used to provide a midterm or year-end grade, or to assess mastery at the end of a unit, is called summative assessment.

Summative assessment has traditionally been the main function of testing in schools, partly because its historical emphasis has been on sorting learners to select those with the highest probability of future success. Selecting students is no longer the principal mission of our schools, as Stiggins and Chappuis (2017) explain.

Schools increasingly need to ensure that learners know how to learn—that they become competent, self-directed learners who are actively involved in their own education.

Formative Assessment

Assessment designed to improve the teaching/learning process is called formative assessment. It consists of the huge variety of methods teachers use to assess and assist learner progress and comprehension during a lesson, course, or unit.

Put another way, formative assessment is assessment for learning; summative assessment is assessment of learning.

Characteristics

Formative assessment is that which is embedded in the teaching–learning process. Much of it is informal rather than structured and formal. It has the following features:

  • It consists of ongoing strategies such as questions, quizzes, and observation of student performances and products.
  • It involves providing frequent and timely feedback aimed directly at improving teaching and learning.

Instead of summarizing achievement with a number or grade (summative assessment), formative assessment provides information that a teacher and learner can put to use before formal grading occurs.

Key to Assessment in Schools

As Hattie (2012) puts it, "The primary function of assessment is to support learning by generating feedback that students can act upon in terms of where they are going, how they are going there, and where they might go next" (p. 126).

Hence, the primary purpose of assessment in schools is (or should be) formative.

Placement Assessment

As the name implies, placement assessment is designed to provide information relating to a learner's placement.

It typically occurs prior to an instructional sequence and looks at the learner's readiness for a lesson, unit, course, or entire program.

Placement assessment often includes diagnostic assessment, which is directed specifically at identifying learning strengths or problems and their underlying causes. For example, diagnostic assessment might be used to determine whether a learner would benefit from being placed in a group that receives remedial instruction in reading or mathematics.

Of course, the fact that both learners and educational emphases are changing does not mean that all teachers stress the formative role of assessment over its summative uses. Some critics point out that many of today's schools still reflect the approaches and emphases of the past. Groshell (2017), for example, notes that although there have been enormous advances in both technology and educational goals, relatively little has changed regarding how schools view learning and how they design the learning environment. Still, there is little doubt that the uses and purposes of educational assessment are changing—and even less doubt that they should.

Note that although assessing, planning, and teaching are shown as distinct activities, they constantly overlap.

Assessment occurs before, during, and after teaching. Planning and modifying approaches to instruction and to learning—all of which rely on good assessment—are continuous, ongoing processes.

Test

In education, the word test typically refers to specific instruments or procedures designed to measure student achievement or progress, or to identify various student characteristics.

Tests typically consist of a sample of behaviors (responses) that serve as a basis for making inferences. For example, a ninth-grade algebra final test does not examine students' complete body of algebraic knowledge or their ability to solve all problems related to the entire course. Instead, the test consists of a representative sample of problems. If a student responds correctly to 84% of the problems, the examiner infers that the student also would have responded correctly to 84% of the entire pool of relevant problems.

Increased Emphasis on Common Standards

Common standards help ensure equity in education across states and prevent a fragmented educational experience for those students who move to new communities that might otherwise have their own unique standards—an increasingly common event in today's more mobile population.

The Common Core State Standards (CCSS)—first introduced across the country in 2009, well before the passage of ESSA in 2015—is one such example of a nationwide effort to develop standards.

Values

The journey of achieving success in college begins with a single step: identifying your personal values. Your personal values are your core beliefs and guiding principles. They shape the roles you play in daily life. They color your interests and passions and frame your thoughts and words. In essence, your values are a compass that help you make decisions and choices.

Another way to recognize the important influence of values is to consider whether you have ever made a decision that you later regretted. Did you reflect on your values prior to making that choice? Sometimes others ask us to do things that are inconsistent with our values. Knowing what you value and making plans accordingly is an important effort to help you stay on track toward your goals.

Try It:

Determining Your Values

To begin to identify some of your personal values, consider the examples listed below. Select the five that you find most important, that bring you the greatest happiness, or that make you feel the most pride. You are encouraged to write in other options that are relevant to you.

Now recall a decision that you have recently made (for example, a smaller decision about how to spend your Saturday or maybe a larger decision about where to apply for part-time work). Did the values you identified through this exercise influence that decision? If so, how?

  • Trello (http://www.trello.com) lets you organize all your obligations in helpful boards so that you can keep track of multiple tasks. You can share them with others (project collaborators), set alerts as reminders, and mark tasks off as you complete them.
  • Todoist (http://www.todoist.com) is a task-management application that can help you organize your tasks. Like Trello, it allows you to collaborate and share your progress on tasks with others.
  • Toggl (https://toggl.com) helps you improve your time management by keeping track of how and where you are spending your time. This allows you to budget your time more effectively as you learn about your current habits.
  • Calm (http://www.calm.com) is a meditation and mindfulness app that provides daily 10-minute meditations geared toward reducing stress and increasing focus. You’ll recall that taking intentional breaks for relaxation helps you manage your mental energy. Calm, and other meditation apps like it, can guide you in beginning this habit.

Priorities

Priorities relate to the word “prior”—to come first or come before. So priorities are the things, people, activities, or even beliefs that you give more of your time or attention to than other things. Return to the values you selected in the Determining Your Values exercise. Of the five values you noted, which is the most important to you? Rank the remaining selected values in order of personal importance as well. Is your ranking influenced by your beliefs and philosophies alone? Or are there external factors that influence their order of importance, such as a current urgency to attend to a loved one, for example?

While values are intrinsic, arising from our own thinking, they may also conflict with one another. Did you take note of that in your own selections and ranking? An example of conflicting values might be valuing achievement and education while also valuing selflessness and family. If these values are among your own, how would you rank achievement if it means asking your family to sacrifice some time with you? How you choose to manage these conflicts reflects your priorities. Your priorities determine where you devote your focus and energies at any given time. While your values generally remain static, the hierarchy of their importance—your priorities—may change over time and are influenced by external factors that may be temporary. Priorities, like values, are key to planning because they help define when you will be best able to allow your educational goal to take precedence and be ranked as more important than some of your other values or obligations, at least for a time. Priorities help you know you’re ready to focus on a particular value or goal.

Reading Systems

Active reading takes time and cannot be rushed. College students employ a number of reading systems, or specific methods of approaching reading, to help them make the most of the time they spend actively reading. This section covers only a portion of those systems, but no matter what system you adopt, devote time, attention, and effort to the process for the most effective results.

SQ3R

One of the most popular systems for active reading is known as SQ3R, which is short for “survey, question, read, recite, and review” the steps in the system. This system is simple to follow and can significantly improve your understanding of what you read.

  • Survey: To survey means to preview information. Before reading a chapter, skim or flip through it and read the bold headings that divide the text into different sections. These headings show you how the chapter is organized and express the main ideas it will cover. As you survey the chapter, pay attention to any hints, tips, or other material shown in the margins or provided in hyperlinks. This text is specially formatted to make it stand out from the rest of the text; it usually summarizes major points or provides helpful information. Next, examine all graphs, pictures, diagrams, and tables. These serve to summarize information in a clear and concise way. Finally, read the introductory and summary paragraphs at the beginning and end of the chapter. These paragraphs indicate what to look for in the chapter and recap the most important information.

  • Question: After your survey, go back to the beginning of the chapter and start reading the headings. As you come to each chapter heading or subheading, change it to a question that features the words “who,” “what,” “where,” “when,” “why,” or “how.” For example, this chapter’s title is “Reading Strategies.” If we change this heading to a question, it might read, “What are reading strategies?” As you read, you will look for information in the chapter that answers that question. Looking for answers to such questions is a form of engaging with the text, and doing so helps you create your own dialogue with it. There is no right or wrong way to form one of these questions. Just apply this method to each chapter heading and subheading as you come across it. Challenging yourself in this way will help you stay interested in what you are reading and improve your understanding of the text.

  • Read:Use the questions you created during the question stage to begin reading actively. Think about what the text means. Because you are reading for information that you may need to retrieve later— for either an exam or a written assignment—develop a system for recording information as you read it. Underline or highlight information that answers your question or that you think is important. Or you can use an asterisk (*), an exclamation point (!), or a question mark (?) to mark passages you think are particularly important to remember.

  • Recite: After you have finished reading a section of text, test whether you actually understand the material by trying to restate it. Reciting does not necessarily mean you say anything out loud. You can recite what you learned to yourself, silently. Or you might tell someone else what you learned, discuss it in a written post, or write a short paragraph in a notebook that summarizes the information. However you choose to “recite,” it is important that you recap what you learned in some way. Think of reciting as a type of self-test to reinforce material in your mind. If you do not clearly understand what you read, go back and review it again. Or ask your instructor or other students to help clarify the meaning.

  • Review: Finally, review the most important information by going back and looking over what you underlined, marked, or highlighted. Reread or type up your handwritten notes. Imagine the sort of quiz questions you might be asked on the material and make sure you have answers to them. When you are finished reading the assigned text, summarize the main points or ideas you think the text is suggesting. Your ultimate goal is to gain a sense of what is valuable in the text.

People turn to the Internet to help them find information for everything from the location of restaurants, to instructions on starting their own business, to possible reasons for that curious rash on their big toe.
