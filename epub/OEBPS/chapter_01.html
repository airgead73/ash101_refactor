Learning Outcomes

After reading this chapter, you should be able to:

  • Describe the benefits of setting goals based on your values and priorities.
  • Demonstrate the ability to set short- and long-term SMART goals.
  • Discuss how getting and staying motivated, being able to overcome roadblocks, and having a support system contribute to achieving your goals.

Research “the key to success” and you will find that sources consistently agree that planning and goal setting are paramount to success. Very few people set out on a road trip purely for the sake of the drive. You must have a destination in mind to know whether you’ve arrived, and a map will indicate whether you’re headed in the right direction. Similarly, you paint a picture of success when you set a goal such that you know what it looks like and can identify when you’ve achieved it. Planning and goal setting help you gauge your progress and know whether you’re on track. Goals and plans also help you decide when it’s time to celebrate or when it’s time to adjust your efforts and redirect your time.

This chapter explores these fundamental principles of success by encouraging you first to identify your values and define the priorities that form the foundation for your goals. You will then learn how to articulate your goals such that your dreams can come to life as a series of real action steps that serve as the framework for your success plan. Because meeting goals requires taking action, you will then learn about and reflect on the motivations and challenges that may drive or impede your progress toward achieving your goals. Successfully identifying your motivations and foreseeing how you can adjust your plan in response to challenges will help you stay on the road to reach your destination and achieve success.

1.1 Identifying Your Values and Priorities

The journey of achieving success in college begins with a single step: identifying your personal values. Your personal values are your core beliefs and guiding principles. They shape the roles you play in daily life. They color your interests and passions and frame your thoughts and words. In essence, your values are a compass that help you make decisions and choices.

Values

Values are the basic beliefs that guide our thinking and actions. Whether we are consciously aware of them or not, values influence both our attitudes and our actions. They help us determine what is important and what makes us happy. It is important to think about and reflect on your values, especially as you make decisions.

Another way to recognize the important influence of values is to consider whether you have ever made a decision that you later regretted. Did you reflect on your values prior to making that choice? Sometimes others ask us to do things that are inconsistent with our values. Knowing what you value and making plans accordingly is an important effort to help you stay on track toward your goals.

Priorities

Priorities relate to the word “prior”—to come first or come before. So priorities are the things, people, activities, or even beliefs that you give more of your time or attention to than other things. Return to the values you selected in the Determining Your Values exercise. Of the five values you noted, which is the most important to you? Rank the remaining selected values in order of personal importance as well. Is your ranking influenced by your beliefs and philosophies alone? Or are there external factors that influence their order of importance, such as a current urgency to attend to a loved one, for example?

While values are intrinsic, arising from our own thinking, they may also conflict with one another. Did you take note of that in your own selections and ranking? An example of conflicting values might be valuing achievement and education while also valuing selflessness and family. If these values are among your own, how would you rank achievement if it means asking your family to sacrifice some time with you? How you choose to manage these conflicts reflects your priorities. Your priorities determine where you devote your focus and energies at any given time. While your values generally remain static, the hierarchy of their importance—your priorities—may change over time and are influenced by external factors that may be temporary. Priorities, like values, are key to planning because they help define when you will be best able to allow your educational goal to take precedence and be ranked as more important than some of your other values or obligations, at least for a time. Priorities help you know you’re ready to focus on a particular value or goal.

1.2 Setting Goals

Have you ever put together a jigsaw puzzle? Many people start by looking for the edge and corner pieces to assemble the border. Some will then group pieces with similar colors, while others just try to fit in new pieces as they pick them up. Regardless of strategy, a jigsaw puzzle is most easily solved when people have a picture to reference. When you know what the picture should look like, you can gauge your progress and avoid making mistakes. If you were to put a puzzle together facedown (cardboard side up, rather than picture side up), you could still connect the pieces, but it would take you much longer to understand how it should fit together. Your attempts, beyond the border, would be mostly by trial and error. Pursuing anything without goals and a plan is like putting together an upside-down puzzle: You can still finish or get to where you’re meant to be, but it will take you much longer to determine your steps along the way.

Goals can be big or small. A goal can range from “I am going to write one extra page tonight,” to “I am going to work to get an A in this course,” all the way to “I am going to graduate in the top of my class so I can start my career with a really good position.” The great thing about goals is that they can include and influence a number of other things that all work toward a much bigger picture. For example, if your goal is to get an A in a certain course, all the reading, studying, and every assignment you do for that course contributes to the larger goal.

Setting goals is frequently talked about, but it is often treated as something abstract. Goal setting is best done with careful thought and planning. This section will explain how you can apply tested techniques to goal setting and what the benefits of each can be.

Set Goals That Motivate You

The first thing to know about goal setting is that a goal is a specific end result you desire. If the goal is not something you are really interested in, there is little motivational drive to achieve it. Think back to when you were much younger and some well-meaning adult set a goal for you—something that didn’t really appeal to you at all. How motivated were you to achieve the goal? More than likely, if you were successful at all in meeting the goal, it was because you were motivated by earning the approval of someone or receiving a possible reward, or you were concerned with avoiding something adverse that might happen if you did not do what you were told. From an honest perspective in that situation, your real goal was based on something else, not the meeting of the goal set for you. To get the most from the goals you set, make sure they are things that you are interested in achieving.

That is not to say you shouldn’t set goals that are supported by other motivations (e.g., If I finish studying by Friday, I can go out on Saturday), but the idea is to be intellectually honest with your goals.

SMART Goals

Goals should also be SMART. A SMART goal is one that is not so vague as to be meaningless, is concrete enough to be achieved, and is time bound (Day & Tosey, 2011; Locke & Latham, 2006). In this case, the word “SMART” is not only a clever description of the type of goal; it is also an acronym that stands for “specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time bound.” These are all desirable traits for your goals because they not only help you plan how to meet the goal, they can also contribute to your decision-making processes during the planning stage.

What does it mean to create SMART goals?

  • Specific: For a goal to be specific, it must be defined enough to actually determine what is to be achieved, by when, and to what standard. A goal of “get a good job when I graduate” is too general. It doesn’t define what a good job is. In fact, it doesn’t even necessarily include a job in your chosen profession. A more specific goal would be something like “be hired as a nurse in a place of employment where it is enjoyable to work and that has room for promotion.”
  • Measurable: The concept of measurable is one that is often overlooked when setting goals. What this means is that the goal should have clearly defined outcomes that are detailed enough to measure and can be used for planning how you will achieve the goal. For example, setting a goal of “doing well in school” is a bit undefined, but making a goal of “graduating with a GPA above 3.0” is measurable and something you can work with. If your goal is measurable, you can know ahead of time how many points you will have to earn on a specific assignment to stay in that range or how many points you will need to make up in the next assignment if you do not do as well as you planned.
  • Achievable: Achievable goals are ones that are reasonable and within your ability to accomplish. While a goal of “make an extra $1 million” by the end of the week is something that would be nice to achieve, the odds that you could make that happen in a single week are not very realistic.
  • Relevant: A relevant goal is one that applies to the situation for which you are planning. For example, if you are planning for success in college, a goal of buying a new TV is not very relevant, but purchasing a new laptop or setting up a space in your home to study would contribute to your success in school.
  • Time bound: In order for a goal to be time bound, you must set a specific time frame in which to achieve the goal. “I will get my paper written by Wednesday” is a time-bound goal. You know when you have to meet the goal (by Wednesday). “I will get my paper written sometime soon” does not help you plan how and when you will accomplish the goal.

SMART goals help you focus on your priorities and manage your time while also providing a means of organizing your thinking and actions into manageable steps. Creating long- and short-term goals will help connect those action steps.

Long-Term Goals

Long-term goals are future goals that often take years to complete. An example of a long-term goal might be to complete a bachelor of arts degree within 4 years. Another example might be purchasing a home or running a marathon. While this chapter focuses on creating a plan for academic success, long-term goals are not exclusive to this area of your life. You might set long-term goals related to your career, fitness, wellness, spirituality, and relationships, among many others. When you set a long-term goal in any aspect of your life, you are demonstrating a commitment to dedicate time and effort toward making progress in that area. Because of this commitment, it is important that your long-term goals be aligned with your values.

Short-Term Goals

Short-term goals are typically things you want to accomplish in the near future. Setting short-term goals helps you consider the necessary steps you’ll need to take, but it also helps chunk a larger effort into smaller, more manageable tasks. Even when your long-term goals are SMART, it’s easier to stay focused and you’ll become less overwhelmed in the process of completing short-term goals.

You might assume that short-term and long-term goals are different goals that vary in the length of time they take to complete. Given this assumption, you might give the example of a long-term goal of learning how to create an app and a short-term goal of remembering to pay your cell phone bill this weekend. These are valid goals, but they don’t exactly demonstrate the intention of short- and long-term goals for the purposes of effective planning.

Instead of just being bound by the difference of time, short-term goals are the action steps that take less time to complete than a long-term goal but that help you work toward your long-term goals. For example, to achieve the long-term goals of determining your best degree option, it might make sense to set the short-term goal of researching what kind of career you’re most interested in pursuing. Or, if you recall that short-term goal of paying your cell phone bill this weekend, perhaps this short-term goal is related to a longer term goal of learning how to manage your budgeting and finances better.

1.3 Achieving Your Goals

The purpose of setting goals is to reframe your dreams into more manageable, achievable action steps. If you do not take these action steps, however, then your goals will remain dreams. It takes determination, passion, and strength of character to persist toward your goals. And, as is the case with longer term goals, it often takes help as well.

As you set your educational goals and develop a plan of action steps, you’ll want to consider why you are motivated to pursue these specific goals. How will you draw on this motivation when you encounter challenges? While your intentions may be focused on the end goal of achieving a degree, are you also motivated and ready to undertake the process of learning? Can you foresee any roadblocks that may stall your progress? How will you navigate them? Who will you rely on to help support you and hold you accountable to your intentions if your motivation falters? As you plan, anticipate challenges and predict how you will use your strengths and resources to overcome them. In doing so, you will be well prepared to achieve your goals.

The sections that follow introduce, in further detail, the considerations of motivation, readiness to learn, ways to navigate roadblocks, and support systems. Inherent in each of these topics is the idea that goals are not met passively. Just as you are learning that planning is a very intentional process, achievement is as well. You must take action, and often repeated actions, to achieve your goals.

Getting and Staying Motivated

Famous physicist Isaac Newton’s first law of motion is an often-cited reference in the discussion of motivation. It states that, unless acted on by an outside force, an object in motion tends to stay in motion and an object at rest tends to stay at rest. If we apply this same law to motivation, the implication is that once you’re already moving in a particular direction, it’s easier to keep going than it is to get started. Getting started often requires some sort of additional push. The beauty of human action is that, unlike a physical force on an object, the push to move can come from within us. This push is what we might think of as motivation. Motivation may also be thought of as the energy form of our values and priorities.

To get motivated, you must set goals that align with your values. This goal-setting process is often the first inspirational push toward action. If you can think of the first moments or days after you’ve set a goal, then perhaps you can relate. You probably feel more focused and more energized to pursue the goal right after you’ve set it. The greater challenge, and where Newton’s metaphor sometimes fails us, is to remain in motion and stay motivated—particularly when pursuing longer term goals. To stay motivated you must maintain the same priorities for a period of time, break your goal into smaller portions that allow you to celebrate successes along the way to your larger goal, and understand where motivation comes from in general so that you can “trick” yourself into being motivated when you’re feeling otherwise inert.

Intrinsic and Extrinsic Motivation

Psychologists who research motivation suggest that there are two primary sources of motivation: intrinsic and extrinsic. Intrinsic motivation is driven by internal factors, such as values, priorities, and interests. Extrinsic motivation is driven by external factors and takes two forms: the desire to seek reward or the desire to avoid punishment.

To illustrate sources of motivation, consider the example of a child who is very fond of art. The child sits for hours cutting, pasting, coloring, and creating. Ask the same child to complete a spelling worksheet for homework, and they may refuse to sit still, refuse to focus, and even throw a tantrum. The child may finally work through the homework, only after the threat of reduced screen time or after the promise of ice cream upon completing the work—or perhaps both! Why will the child focus on art but refuse to sit in the same spot to focus on homework?

In this example, the child is intrinsically motivated to do what they enjoy. Intrinsic motivation is inspired. It is putting in time and effort for the sake of enjoyment and the sake of the activity itself. The child receives pleasure and satisfaction from doing what they like and, therefore, is intrinsically motivated to pursue it. While the child is clearly not intrinsically motivated to complete their spelling homework, they may become extrinsically motivated to complete it when threatened with punishment or promised a reward. Extrinsic motivation is putting in time and effort in order to avoid punishment or receive a reward. In either case, extrinsic motivation involves a push from external factors.

Adults are similarly motivated by intrinsic and extrinsic factors, so you will likely see parallels between your own thinking and this illustration. While an idealist may wish that both children and adults were intrinsically motivated to learn for the sake of learning, extrinsic motivation plays a vital role in fostering competition and in helping you maintain the order of your priorities to see a goal through to completion, even if your interests have shifted or changed.

You can likely already define what inspires and interests you, or provides you with intrinsic motivation. Before moving on in your reading, take a moment to consider the extrinsic motivators that may influence you. If you begin to lose your inspiration or enjoyment, you can actually use extrinsic motivators to “trick” your own thinking. You may find that you can persist through writing a college essay with the promise of an ice-cream reward as well.

Readiness to Learn

Because enrolling in a college or university degree program is a big decision—one of the most significant in a lifetime, for many—it’s really important to be able to answer yes to the question “Am I ready to learn?” Researchers have identified various sets of conditions, known as contexts, that stimulate learning. In this section we’ll cover four particular contexts to help you understand what motivates and gives you confidence to learn. As you read about each context, ask yourself, “Is this context (or set of conditions) similar to mine as I pursue my degree online?” Or, “Do I feel comfortable thinking of my online learning decision in this context?”

When you can answer yes to these questions, you are acquiring a level of self-knowledge and confidence about what motivates you to proceed toward your goal of a college or university degree. That is, you are not saying “I should” get a university degree or “I want” to get a degree. Instead, you are recognizing (and saying) “I know I’m ready to begin . . . I feel comfortable identifying myself with a particular supportive context or perspective—one that motivates me to learn and makes my goal realistic and highly desirable.”

As you review the following “readiness to learn contexts,” you may find particular aspects that describe your situation in more than one context, and you may not feel that you completely fit into any one of the four. That’s fine. Human decisions are normally motivated by more than one source. But you should determine which of these contexts for learning supports you in a primary way and which supports you in a secondary way.

Contexts That Motivate Learning

Based on the research of Malcolm Knowles (1984), the following are four “distinct contexts” that stimulate adults to learn. We have cited a student experience to illustrate each one. Read each short narrative carefully and think about your own situation as you proceed.

Practical Context

I need to know in order to cope with things I face.

Sara’s story: I’m pretty open to learning, but I’m most enthusiastic about it when I know that I’m going to gain something practical from it. I’m not really interested in just being told, “You gotta learn this,” without knowing in advance how it’s going to benefit me. That doesn’t mean I always have to apply what I learn immediately. I’m comfortable with knowing it will be useful sometime in the future. When I think about situations in my family and at work that make demands on me, I feel that I’m handling them, but I’d like to be able to cope with them better. I’d like to be sure about the approaches I’m taking and decisions I’m making. I’d like solutions to problems I face.

Personal Context

I want to do this myself and accomplish important goals.

Chris’s story: When it comes to learning, I see myself positioned in the starting blocks with a clear path stretched out in front of me, a path that will get me to the finish line. Sure, it would be a bit foolish to say, “I see the finish line clearly,” because I’m realistic enough to know that circumstances, conflicts, and surprises always happen to slow you down. But what motivates me to learn is being able to say, “There’s a goal out there that I can glimpse, and I’m committed to reaching it.” I know what it is, and I’m on my way toward getting there. Learning makes me feel better about myself, and I definitely think that the learning quest that I’m on will improve the quality of my life and my future.

Experiential Context

I’m at a point that I can pull things together from experience.

Fred’s story: A lot of people talk primarily about the future when they explain their interest in learning. I realize that it’s important to plan for the future. But I like to consider the past, too, and my significant insights from past experiences. Real-life experiences have informed me accurately about many things, allowing me to develop a solid foundation for forming values and being confident in what I do and think. I may have to unlearn some things, but I’m open to that. My ideal learning environment is one in which I can consider what I’ve already learned from life experience, reflect on it, and then adapt it to a new situation—discovering new meaning and competence in the process.

Idealistic Context

I want to explore ideas, theories, and concepts to experience discovery of something new.

Jamie’s story: Researchers call me a cognitive learner. Those who don’t use such technical terms label me as a person more interested in theory than in practice. I can accept that, but I know that practice can’t be avoided entirely. In any given situation, what triggers my interest in learning is learning for its own sake. I know grades, diplomas, and new jobs are desirable aspects of the formal educational journey, but they don’t motivate me nearly as much as the pleasure of discovering something new or learning something that may not have utilitarian value. For me, there’s joy just in expanding my mind, and I don’t plan to abandon this learning process as long as I live.

Navigating Roadblocks

Let’s say that you’re a generally punctual person. You’ve made an arrangement to meet a friend in the next town for a cup of coffee. You’ve been to the coffee shop before, so you know the route and how long it will take to get there. You leave on time, but there’s unexpected construction traffic along the way that causes a delay. You arrive 20 minutes late, and your friend has already finished the coffee they ordered when they arrived on time. Because you are allergic to dairy, you order a soy latte, only to find out that the coffee shop is out of soy. When you finally settle on a cup of tea, you realize you’ve forgotten your wallet. Thankfully, your friend grabs this one for you and says you’ll owe them next time. You sit and chat, and you begin to calm down from this series of frustrations.

Perhaps you’ve encountered some of these exact frustrations in your own life. Each frustration in this illustration is minor, of course, but they serve as a good reminder that even when we have the skills, plan, motivation, and best of intentions, roadblocks (like construction traffic) can derail and disappoint. This simple illustration also provides an example of the skills you need to navigate and overcome roadblocks. Continuing to press on through the traffic and continue toward your goal despite the delay demonstrates the mindset to overcome negative thinking. Selecting a tea when soy isn’t available demonstrates the ability to manage change. Making a payment arrangement with your friend and enjoying the calming effect of their company demonstrates the ability to ask for help and build and rely on your support systems.

The next sections clarify these concepts as they relate to roadblocks you may encounter as you work toward your planned goal, but as you read further, it is important to keep in mind that you already have what it takes to navigate and overcome problems even when facing higher stakes goals than a coffee date.

Combating Negative Bias and Stereotype Threat

Negative bias and stereotype threat are two psychological tendencies that can not only adversely affect learning but can set up roadblocks that may prevent you from achieving many goals.

Negative bias is the psychological trait of focusing on the negative aspects of a situation rather than the positive. An example of this in a learning environment would be earning a 95% score on an assignment but obsessing over the 5% of the points that were missed. Another example would be worrying and thinking negative thoughts about yourself over a handful of courses in which you did not do as well as in others—so much so that you begin to doubt your abilities altogether.

Unfortunately, this is a human tendency that can often overwhelm a student. As a pure survival mechanism, it does have its usefulness in that it reminds us to be wary of behaviors that can result in undesirable outcomes. Imagine that as a child playing outside, you have seen dozens if not hundreds of bees over the years. But once, out of all those other times, you were stung by a single bee. Now every time you see a bee you recall the sting, and you now have a negative bias toward bees in general. Whenever possible, you avoid bees altogether.

It is easy to see how this psychological system could be beneficial in those types of situations, but it can be a hindrance in learning since a large part of the learning process often involves failure on early attempts. Recognizing this is a key to overcoming negative bias. Another way to combat negative bias is to purposefully focus on successes and to acknowledge earlier attempts that fail as just a part of the learning process.

What follows are a few methods for overcoming negative bias and negative self-talk. Each focuses on being aware of any negative attitude or emphasizing the positive aspects in a situation.

  • Be aware of any negative bias. Keep an eye out for any time you find yourself focusing on some negative aspect, whether regarding your own abilities or a specific situation. Whenever you recognize that you are exhibiting a negative bias toward something, stop and look for the positive parts of the experience. Remind yourself that any lack of success is only temporary, and focus on what you have learned that gets you closer to your goal.
  • Focus on the positive before you begin. While reversing the impact of negative bias on your learning is helpful, it can be even more useful to prevent it in the first place. One way to do this is to look for the positives before you begin a task. An example of this would be receiving early feedback for an assignment you are working on. To accomplish this, you can often ask your instructor or one of your classmates to look over your work and provide some informal comments. If the feedback is positive, then you know you are on the right track. That is useful information. Feedback that indicates you need to make a number of corrections and adjustments is even more useful; you can use it to improve the assignment for a better final grade. In either case accurate feedback is what you really want most, and both outcomes are positive for you.
  • Keep a gratitude and accomplishment journal. Again, the tendency to recall and overemphasize the negative instances while ignoring or forgetting about the positive outcomes is the nature of negative bias. Sometimes we need a little help remembering the positives, and we can prompt our memories by keeping a journal. Just as in a diary, the idea is to keep a flowing record of the positive things that happen, the lessons you learned from instances that were “less than successful,” and all accomplishments you make toward learning. In your journal you can write or paste anything that you appreciated or that has positive outcomes. Whenever you are not feeling up to a challenge or when negative bias is starting to wear on you, you can look over your journal to remind yourself of previous accomplishments in the face of adversity.

Like negative bias, stereotype threat is a similar psychological phenomenon that can impact your performance and ability to achieve your goals. Essentially, stereotype threat occurs when a member of a social group feels at risk of conforming to stereotypes about that group. Stereotypes are the assumptions about different groups (such as different ethnic, racial, or cultural groups) that characterize that group as a whole, rather than accounting for individual differences. Research on stereotypes indicates that a widespread belief about a group’s ability to perform well on a task can negatively impact the performance of an individual who identifies as a member of that group (Steele & Aronson, 1995; Aronson et al., 2002). Whereas negative bias may arise from your own habits or ways of thinking, stereotype threat demonstrates the possible impact of others’ thinking or beliefs on your performance. Stereotype threat can result in stress and anxiety that actually do negatively impact performance, as well as increased pressure to prove the stereotype wrong.

The first step to combat stereotype threat is to be aware of this phenomenon. To avoid having others’ expectations become self-fulfilling, it is important to recognize your strengths and skills as an individual and question when group labels are applied when related to academic performance. Following role models who have overcome stereotype threat can also serve as a source of inspiration that can keep you focused on possibilities for your own future as well.

Expecting and Managing Change

After you’ve devoted time to planning, it can be frustrating when circumstances unexpectedly change. Change can be the result of internal or external factors. Internal factors are those that you have control over. They may include indecision, changing your mind about a situation after receiving new information, or recognizing that something is not a good fit for your values and goals. Though change resulting from internal factors can be stressful, it is often easier to accept and to navigate because you know why the change must occur. You can plan for a change and make even better decisions for your path when the reason for change is something over which you have control!

External factors that necessitate change are often harder to plan for and accept. Some external factors may include financial concerns, your health or the health of a loved one, or other family circumstances. Change caused by external factors can be frustrating. Because external factors are often unexpected, when you encounter them you’ll often have to spend more time changing your plans or even revising your goals before you’ll feel as though you’re back on track.

It is important to recognize that change, whether internal or external, is inevitable. You can probably think of an example of a time when you had to change your plans due to unforeseen circumstances. Perhaps it’s a situation as simple as canceling a date with friends because of an obligation to babysit a sibling. Even though this simple example would not have had long-term consequences, you can probably recall a feeling of disappointment. It’s okay to feel disappointed; however, you’ll also want to recognize that you can manage your response to changing circumstances. You can ask yourself the following questions:

  • What can I control in this situation?
  • Do I need to reconsider my values?
  • Do I need to reconsider my goals?
  • Do I need to change my plans as a result of this new information or these new circumstances?
  • What resources, tools, or people are available to assist me in revising my plans?

When encountering change, it helps to remember that decision-making and planning are continuous processes. In other words, active individuals are always engaged in decision-making, setting new plans, and revising old plans. This continuous process is not always the result of major life-changing circumstances, either. Oftentimes, we need to make changes simply because we’ve learned some new information that causes a shift in our plans. Planning, like learning, is an ongoing lifetime process.

Asking for Help

There are a number of individuals who can help you plan your path, but keep in mind that your path is ultimately your own. Some students make the mistake of taking too much advice when planning and making decisions. They may forgo their values and goals for others’ values and goals for them. Or they may mistakenly trust advice that comes from well-meaning but ill-informed sources.

In other cases, students may grapple with unfamiliar college paperwork and technology with little assistance. It’s important to know that seeking help is a strength, not a weakness, particularly when that help comes from well-informed individuals who have your best interests in mind. When you share your goals and include others in your planning, you develop both a support network and a system of personal accountability. Being held accountable for your goals means that others are also tracking your progress and are interested in seeing you succeed. When you are working toward a goal and sticking to a plan, it’s important to have unconditional cheerleaders in your life as well as folks who keep pushing you to stay on track, especially if they see you stray. It’s important to know who in your life can play these roles.

Support Systems for Learning Success

Have you ever heard the saying “It takes a village. . .”? That proverb is applicable to college students as well. There will indeed be times when you may feel overwhelmed, frustrated, isolated, or simply just tired as you complete your program of study. That is where a strong support network will come in. Your personal support network could consist of your family, friends, coworkers, and community. Their support, understanding, and continual motivation is good, of course, but they may also provide you more direct assistance as well. Letting these special people in your life know specifically what you need and how they can help you be successful in college is definitely a conversation to have early and often.

Support From Family and Friends

Because of the duration of our relationships, the amount of time we spend to together, and our emotional closeness, family and friends tend to be our greatest influences. This influence can be negative and can serve as a strong motivator for an individual to strive for something different from the examples set by peers and family. Or, and as is hopefully the case for your experience, this influence can serve as a strong positive foundation for your support network. Or perhaps, as you reflect on your own circle, it is a mix of these influences. In any case it is important to identify family and friends who support your goals.

Family and friends know you. They are familiar with your strengths and weaknesses. They have witnessed what triggers your stress, how you respond to stress, and how you cope. Because they have this knowledge, if family and friends are open to sharing, they can often help you become more self-aware, make better decisions about how and where to channel your efforts, and better manage work–life balance and stress. Conversely, you also know your family and friends. You know who you can contact to answer your questions, talk through your thinking about an assignment, or provide words of encouragement. You know who will call you out when you’re procrastinating. You know who will be understanding of your need to make fewer social plans or to “hide” in a quiet space to study for a while. You also know who will distract you and get you off track, even if they have good intentions or have immediate needs that they need you to fulfill (such as with a child or an elder in your care).

Consider these individuals in your immediate circle as you set your goals. Open early lines of communication with family and friends who will understand and help give you space and support, but also open early lines of communication with family and friends who might not yet understand. Share what you need from these individuals so that you can succeed. If you are able to, set specific expectations, particularly with those for whom you provide care, about how often you can respond to needs. Perhaps you’ll be an inspirational support for them as well!

Support From Faculty

Solid student–faculty relationships can be foundational to a successful college experience. College students are sometimes surprised to discover that instructors enjoy getting to know students. The human dimension of college really matters, and as a student you are an important part of your instructor’s world. Most instructors are happy to work with you via digital messages, phone, or online discussion forums.

One of the many benefits of communicating with instructors is that it can help you feel more comfortable in college and more connected to the college culture. Research has indicated that students who are connected and feel as though they are a part of the college community are less likely to become dispirited and drop out (Tinto, 2012). In an online learning environment, communication with instructors (and classmates, of course!) is one of the best ways to develop this connection.

Communicating with instructors is also a valuable way to learn about an academic field or a career. Maybe you don’t know for sure what you want to major in or what people with a degree in your chosen major actually do after college. Most instructors will share information and insights with you.

Think about what being “educated” truly means: how one thinks, understands society and the world, and responds to problems and new situations. Much of this learning occurs outside of the formal class. Communicating with your instructors and college leaders can be among your most meaningful experiences in college.

Communication Skills: Interacting With Faculty

College students enter into a world surrounded by people who are very invested in student success. Faculty are students’ greatest champions and look for ways to help them learn and prosper. Just think—faculty loved college so much they came back . . . or never left. They have years of experience navigating the college scene and can provide you with a wealth of information when you run up against a barrier. Knowing that should give you some comfort—you have an amazing team in your corner to help you move toward your goals. Sometimes students don’t want to impose on faculty members or bother them with questions or concerns. Actually, faculty welcome questions and want students to ask for clarification if they are confused about an assignment or subject-related question.

Getting along with instructors and communicating well begins with attitude. As experts in their field, instructors deserve respect. Remember that a college education is a collaborative process that works best when students and instructors communicate freely in an exchange of ideas, information, and perspectives. So while you should respect your instructors, there is no need to fear them. As you get to know them better, you’ll learn their personalities and find appropriate ways to talk to them. Below are some guidelines for getting along with and communicating with your instructors:

  • Prepare before talking with the instructor. Go over your notes on readings and assignments and write down your specific questions. You’ll feel more comfortable, and the instructor will appreciate you being organized.

  • Respect the instructor’s time. In addition to teaching, college instructors participate in committees, conduct research and other professional work, and have personal lives. It’s not appropriate to arrive late to a scheduled appointment and expect the instructor to stay late to talk with you.

  • Don’t try to fool an instructor. Insincere praise or making excuses for not doing an assignment will rarely play in your favor (they’ve heard it all before!). Nor is it a good idea to act like you’re “too cool” to take your classwork seriously—another attitude that’s sure to put off an instructor. To earn your instructor’s respect, do the work, genuinely participate in class discussions, and show respect—and the instructor will be happy to work with you should you need some extra help.

  • Be professional when talking to an instructor. You can be cordial and friendly, but it’s ideal to keep it professional and on an adult level. Unless the instructor has already asked you to address them as “Dr. _______,” “Ms. _______,” or Mr. _______,” or something similar, it’s appropriate to say “Professor _______.” Use this individualized access to your professors wisely and ask genuine questions that help you clarify points you didn’t understand from your readings, assignments, or discussions. Be prepared to accept constructive criticism in a professional way, without taking it personally or complaining.

  • If you need more help, go back again and discuss the issue with your professor. This interest in your own success goes a long way in helping faculty members know you better as an individual learner.

Support From Work Relationships

Maintaining a structure of open communication and compromise can be useful in developing awareness of your educational goals in your work relationships and in identifying support. Often, you can arrange adjustments in such areas as scheduling and team responsibility assignments if you engage your colleagues and supervisors in open communication about the workload and timelines you are facing in your university courses. The common understanding resulting from such conversations creates a basis for maintaining work productivity and replacing frustration with support.

In addition to aiding in prioritizing your time and workload, work relationships can also provide you support specific to the content and skills that you’re learning in your courses. Your workplace has likely provided you with life experiences and opportunities to learn specific technical skills that provide the foundation for new learning. Having prior knowledge about a related subject can help you learn and remember new information. Making connections and practicing what you’re learning helps reinforce that learning. For example, coursework that involves computer skills, hones teamwork and leadership skills, improves public speaking, and develops your professional communication skills may directly or indirectly relate to your current work. The ability to practice these skills both in coursework and at work provides you an advantage.

Sharing what you’re learning with trusted colleagues also provides opportunity to practice and apply new knowledge. It may also provide the added benefit of helping employers know (if they’re not already aware) that you’re a goal-directed and motivated individual. Recognition of your potential and the skills that you’re learning may provide you further opportunities for future projects or promotions.

Conclusion

This chapter began by describing the guideposts for planning: values, priorities, and goals. These guideposts are interrelated and deeply individual, since they stem from your core beliefs, philosophies, and inspirations. While your goals may differ from others’, there are universal best practices for setting goals. Recall that a goal remains a dream unless you actually take the steps to achieve it. The primary purpose of articulating SMART goals and developing short-term milestones toward your longer term goal is to make that goal more manageable and to help you become and stay motivated as you see your progress.

Keep in mind that as you track this progress, you may need to make changes to your plan. You may need to “trick” yourself with rewards or engage in friendly competition with study partners, relying on extrinsic motivators to reignite your motivation. You can refer back to the section on managing change to consider how you might respond if you face such challenges. Finally, recall that others can both help you plan and hold you accountable to your goals, but only you define your values, so feel confident in sticking to them!

Key Terms

extrinsic motivation 

A desire to do something that is driven by external factors. This type of motivation takes two forms: the desire to seek reward or the desire to avoid punishment.

intrinsic motivation 

A desire to do something that is driven by internal factors, such as values, priorities, and interests.

negative bias 

The psychological trait of focusing on the negative aspects of a situation rather than the positive.

priorities 

The things, people, activities, or even beliefs that you give more of your time or attention to than other things.

SMART goal 

A goal that is specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time bound.

stereotype threat 

A situation in which a member of a social group feels at risk of conforming to stereotypes about that group.

values 

The basic beliefs that guide our thinking and actions.
